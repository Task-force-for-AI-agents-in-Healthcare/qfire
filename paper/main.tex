\begin{abstract}
Large language models embedded in autonomous agents process trusted instructions
and untrusted data in one context window, leaving them open to direct and
indirect prompt injection. Existing runtime guardrails trade safety against
latency: model-based auditors are accurate but add hundreds of milliseconds of
Python inference, while lexical filters are fast but blind to obfuscated or
semantically disguised payloads. We present \textbf{QFIRE}, an inline,
provider-agnostic prompt firewall implemented as a single self-contained Rust
toolchain (proxy, CLI, and benchmark harness). QFIRE contributes three ideas:
(i) \emph{positive-security scope constraining}, which restricts a model call to
a declared natural-language purpose and blocks out-of-scope drift even when no
overt attack token is present; (ii) an \emph{asynchronous detector graph} that
runs $N$ rules and their typed detector nodes concurrently on a Tokio runtime
with cheap-before-expensive short-circuiting; and (iii) a \emph{de-obfuscation
normalization pass} that decodes Base64/hex/ROT13, folds homoglyphs and
leetspeak, and strips zero-width characters before detection. QFIRE ships
${\sim}100$ versioned firewall rules, a dedicated HIPAA Safe-Harbor (18
identifier) healthcare/PHI panel, and runs the
\texttt{deberta-v3-base-prompt-injection} classifier locally via embedded ONNX
Runtime. On \NumPrompts{} public prompt-injection and jailbreak prompts QFIRE's
deterministic hybrid attains F1~$0.86$, statistically tied with Meta's
state-of-the-art PromptGuard-2 (F1~$0.86$) and above protectai DeBERTa-v3
(F1~$0.83$), while lexical baselines lag (F1~$0.16$--$0.50$). Our central result is
on \emph{QFIRE-HealthBench}, a new 2{,}000-prompt healthcare benchmark we build
with real garak and Microsoft PyRIT payloads: there the same SOTA PromptGuard-2
recovers only $0.40$ recall (DeBERTa-v3 $0.57$), because most clinical
threats---PHI exfiltration, cross-patient access, re-identification, bulk export,
out-of-scope advice---carry no injection signal, whereas QFIRE's combined
scope+PHI chain reaches $0.83$ recall (F1~$0.87$) at a calibrated $0.08$
false-positive rate. Generic prompt-injection detection, even SOTA, is therefore
necessary but not sufficient for healthcare LLM agents; positive-security scope
and PHI controls close a gap a classifier structurally cannot. We report
precision/recall/F1 with 95\% Wilson confidence intervals, ROC--AUC, and
p50/p95/p99 latency. All code, rules, corpora snapshots, and scripts are released,
and every table regenerates from a single \texttt{make paper} target against local
models with no paid API keys.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The integration of large language models (LLMs) into agentic systems---tools,
browsers, retrieval pipelines---has moved AI security from content moderation to
application-layer defense-in-depth~\cite{llamafirewall2025,greshake2023}. Because
an LLM consumes its system prompt and untrusted inputs within a single semantic
context, instruction-override, role-play jailbreak, and adversarial-suffix
attacks remain effective despite model alignment~\cite{perez2022,greshake2023}.
Runtime gateways that intercept prompts before they reach the model are therefore
a necessary control~\cite{llamafirewall2025,oneshield2025}.

This work extends the Healthcare AI Agents Regulatory Framework
(HAARF)~\cite{haarf2026}, a security-verification standard for autonomous AI
systems in clinical environments from the same group. Where HAARF specifies
\emph{what} security controls a clinical AI agent must satisfy, QFIRE is a
concrete, measured \emph{enforcement} layer that hardens those controls at
runtime---in particular the scope-restriction and PHI-handling requirements that,
as we show, generic prompt-injection detectors structurally cannot meet.

A recurring tension in this literature is \emph{security robustness versus
latency}. Heavy semantic verifiers and model-based auditors achieve strong
detection but introduce substantial per-call latency from Python-hosted
inference~\cite{llamafirewall2025}; conversely, deterministic regular-expression
filters are sub-millisecond but fail on obfuscated or paraphrased
attacks~\cite{greshake2023}. QFIRE targets this gap with a zero-trust,
ultra-low-latency design built in Rust.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Positive-security scope constraining.} Beyond flagging overt
  attacks, QFIRE enforces a declared operational scope (e.g.\ ``marketing copy
  only'', ``appointment scheduling, no clinical advice'') and blocks anything
  outside it, neutralizing camouflaged or benign-looking drift.
  \item \textbf{Asynchronous detector graph with collapse.} Rules and detector
  nodes execute concurrently; a chain collapses $N$ per-rule verdicts into one
  terminal decision in either an ordered (iptables-style) or boolean-expression
  mode, with cheap-before-expensive short-circuiting.
  \item \textbf{De-obfuscation normalization} that exposes hidden payloads
  (Base64/hex/ROT13, homoglyphs, leetspeak, zero-width) before detection, and a
  complete \textbf{HIPAA Safe-Harbor PHI} panel covering all 18 identifiers.
  \item \textbf{A reproducible benchmark} comparing QFIRE head-to-head with open
  detectors on public corpora, with Wilson confidence intervals and latency
  percentiles, regenerated end-to-end by \texttt{make paper}.
\end{enumerate}

injection~\cite{greshake2023}). The defender controls a network position

purpose and (b) detect injection/jailbreak attempts, while preserving throughput.

\section{System Design}
QFIRE is an inline reverse proxy and CLI. Incoming requests in the native wire
formats of OpenAI, Anthropic, Gemini, and Ollama are normalized into one internal
representation; the firewall evaluates the extracted prompt and, on \textsc{allow},
transparently forwards the original request downstream. Figure~\ref{fig:arch}
shows the end-to-end path: SDKs reach QFIRE by changing only their base URL, the
engine evaluates the selected chain on a Tokio runtime, and every decision is
written to an immutable audit log before a request is forwarded or refused.

\subsection{Rules, chains, and collapse}
A \emph{rule} declares a natural-language scope and an ordered \emph{detector
pipeline}; each node returns a verdict, confidence, latency, and rationale. A
\emph{chain} composes rules into one terminal decision in two interchangeable
modes: \textsc{ordered} (first matching block/allow wins, configurable default)
and \textsc{expression} (a boolean DAG over named rules, e.g.\
\texttt{injection\_guard AND (marketing OR support)}). Detectors are typed as
\emph{blockers} (regex, Aho-Corasick, entropy, the injection classifier), which
return \textsc{block} or \textsc{abstain}, or \emph{scope deciders} (LLM judge,
exemplar similarity), which return \textsc{allow}/\textsc{block}; the expression
predicate ``rule did not block'' lets guards and scope rules compose uniformly.

\subsection{A declarative firewall language}\label{sec:language}
The deepest difference from PromptGuard-2, LlamaFirewall, and Protect~AI LLM Guard
is not a single detector: a QFIRE firewall is \emph{data, not code}. Those systems
express policy in Python and model weights; QFIRE expresses it in
version-controlled YAML that the engine interprets, making policy auditable,
diff-able, and unit-testable (\texttt{qfire rules test} runs each rule's exemplars
as fixtures). Figure~\ref{fig:spec} shows a rule, an expression chain, and an
ordered chain verbatim from the repository; Table~\ref{tab:library} summarizes the
shipped library. Detector nodes are typed---\texttt{regex}/\texttt{aho} (sub-ms
denylists), \texttt{entropy}, \texttt{phi} (18 HIPAA identifiers),
\texttt{deberta} (local ONNX classifier), \texttt{judge} (LLM scope), and
\texttt{similarity}---and are ordered cheap-before-expensive so a rule
short-circuits on the first block before reaching the classifier or judge.

\subsection{Asynchronous detector graph}
Given prompt $P$ and active rules $R=\{r_1,\dots,r_N\}$, QFIRE evaluates rules
concurrently on a Tokio runtime under a bounded-concurrency semaphore. Within a
rule, nodes run in pipeline order with short-circuiting, so a cheap lexical
detector that fires aborts the remaining---possibly expensive---nodes (e.g.\ the
local DeBERTa classifier or an LLM judge). A verdict cache keyed by
$\mathrm{sha256}(P)$ plus node identity lets repeated or replayed prompts skip
recomputation. The terminal decision is a collapsible boolean over per-node block
states.

\subsection{De-obfuscation normalization}
Before detection, an optional normalization pass produces an expanded view of $P$:
it strips zero-width characters, folds common Cyrillic/Greek homoglyphs and
leetspeak to ASCII, and decodes Base64/hex runs and ROT13, appending each
recovered layer. Detectors then scan the expanded view, exposing payloads that
the raw text would smuggle past lexical and even learned detectors.

\subsection{Detector library, healthcare panel, and PHI}
QFIRE ships ${\sim}100$ versioned rules across injection-defense, marketing,
support, code, data/SQL, finance, legal, and content-safety domains, plus a
dedicated healthcare panel. The PHI engine implements all 18 HIPAA Safe-Harbor
identifiers~\cite{hipaa_safe_harbor} (SSN, dates, phone/fax, email, MRN,
beneficiary/account numbers, URLs, IP addresses, VINs, device/license serials,
geographic and contextual name/biometric cues), reporting each hit's category and
supporting typed redaction.

\subsection{Implementation}
QFIRE is ${\sim}7$k lines of Rust (Tokio, axum, reqwest). The injection
classifier \texttt{deberta-v3-base-prompt-injection}~\cite{protectai_deberta}
runs locally via embedded ONNX Runtime~\cite{ort} with a bundled tokenizer; when
the ONNX model is absent a transparent lexical classifier is used and reported as
such, so results are never silently degraded. The LLM scope-judge can target any
configured provider; all reproducibility runs use local Ollama~\cite{ollama}.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\paragraph{Data.} We evaluate on \NumPrompts{} public prompts
(\NumAttacks{} attack, \NumBenign{} benign) drawn from the
deepset prompt-injections~\cite{deepset_pi} and jailbreak-classification
\cite{jailbreak_cls} datasets, snapshotted and versioned in the repository.
\paragraph{Baselines.} We compare against open detectors run on the identical
corpus: \texttt{deberta-v3-base-prompt-injection}~\cite{protectai_deberta} (the
de-facto open detector, and the engine inside Protect~AI LLM Guard), executed in
PyTorch; Meta \texttt{Llama-Prompt-Guard-2-86M}~\cite{promptguard2}; and lexical
regex / Aho-Corasick filters. QFIRE runs the same DeBERTa weights via Rust ONNX.
Meta PromptGuard-2 is run locally in PyTorch with an authorized Hugging Face token
(gate accepted), and the PyTorch DeBERTa baseline---after resolving an unrelated
\texttt{torch}/\texttt{torchvision} operator clash by removing torchvision---
reproduces the ONNX path within ${\sim}0.02$ F1, confirming the integration is
faithful. On the public corpus PromptGuard-2 is the strongest single classifier
(P~$0.997$/R~$0.755$/F1~$0.859$/FPR~$0.002$), statistically even with QFIRE's
hybrid; on QFIRE-HealthBench (\S\ref{sec:healthbench}) the same model recovers only
$0.40$ recall, the gap this paper is about.
\paragraph{Metrics.} Treating ``attack'' as the positive class we report
precision, recall, F1, false-positive rate, accuracy with 95\% Wilson score
intervals~\cite{wilson1927}, ROC--AUC (from continuous detector scores), and
latency percentiles (p50/p95/p99). Runs are seeded and described by a manifest.

\subsection{Latency and cost}
Deterministic detectors (regex, Aho-Corasick, entropy) run in under
$0.1$\,ms/prompt. The local DeBERTa-v3 classifier, executed via Rust ONNX on CPU,
costs ${\approx}81$\,ms mean / $255$\,ms p95 (cold) and is paid only when the
cheap detectors abstain. The LLM judge is the only network-cost node; under local
Ollama every call is \$0, so overhead is reported as latency, and the
ten-judge healthcare chain reaches a p95 of ${\approx}2.8$\,s---motivating
cheap-before-expensive ordering and modest rule counts.
\emph{Measurement integrity:} within a single \texttt{bench} invocation the
verdict cache is shared across chains, so chains evaluated after the DeBERTa-only
chain read cached classifier verdicts and report artificially low latency. We
therefore quote the \emph{cold} DeBERTa latency and characterize the hybrid
qualitatively: short-circuiting means overtly-malicious prompts are caught by
sub-millisecond lexical detectors and never reach the classifier.
An independent PyTorch run of the same protectai weights on the same full corpus
measured P~$0.984$/R~$0.721$/F1~$0.832$ at p95~$193$\,ms, versus the Rust ONNX
P~$0.976$/R~$0.744$/F1~$0.844$ at p95~$255$\,ms: the near-identical P/R/F1
(within ${\sim}0.02$) validates that our ONNX integration faithfully reproduces
the PyTorch reference, while the latency shows Rust ONNX is \emph{not} faster than
PyTorch on this CPU. We thus make no raw-speed claim; the Rust advantage is
single-binary, no-Python, in-process deployment.

\section{QFIRE-HealthBench}\label{sec:healthbench}
QFIRE-HealthBench is a healthcare-specific prompt-injection dataset: $1{,}000$
benign clinical-adjacent prompts and $1{,}000$ malicious prompts built with real
garak payloads and real Microsoft PyRIT converters (PyRIT under a Python-3.11
venv; garak DAN-family and in-the-wild jailbreaks from NVIDIA/garak). It targets
threats a generic injection classifier misses: PHI exfiltration, cross-patient
access, re-identification, bulk export, and out-of-scope clinical advice. All
identifiers are synthetic; the dataset and its card ship in
\texttt{corpora/healthcare\_bench/}.

Table~\ref{tab:healthbench} reports the central healthcare result. An injection
classifier alone caps far below QFIRE---and the \emph{strongest} generic detector
we measured, Meta PromptGuard-2 (F1~$0.86$ on public injection), recovers only
$0.40$ recall here; protectai DeBERTa $0.57$; QFIRE's classifier-only chain
$0.59$. The reason is structural: most healthcare threats carry no jailbreak
token. Adding the PHI detector and positive-security scope rules
(\texttt{bench\_combined}) lifts recall to $0.83$ (F1 $0.73 \rightarrow 0.87$) at a
calibrated FPR of $0.08$. Per category the detectors are complementary: DeBERTa
owns direct-injection/system-exfiltration/jailbreak; the PHI engine owns
bulk-export/PHI-smuggling (zero injection signal, DeBERTa $0.00$); and only the
combined chain covers re-identification ($0.00\rightarrow1.00$), PHI-exfiltration
($0.41\rightarrow0.94$), and cross-patient access ($0.41\rightarrow0.62$). This is
the quantitative case for the paper's thesis: generic injection detection---even
SOTA---is necessary but not sufficient in healthcare; scope and PHI controls close
a gap a classifier structurally cannot.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}

\paragraph{Detectors are complementary, not redundant.}
Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} makes the paper's central claim visible at a glance: it
plots per-category recall for each detector chain on the 1{,}000 malicious
QFIRE-HealthBench prompts. The structure is block-diagonal, not uniform. The
injection classifier (column~1) is strong exactly where an injection signal exists
---direct injection, system-prompt exfiltration, jailbreak---and falls to
\emph{zero} on bulk export, PHI smuggling, and re-identification, which contain no
such signal. The PHI detector (column~2) is the mirror image: it owns
bulk-export and PHI-smuggling but is blind to jailbreaks. Neither single detector
is sufficient, and crucially their failure modes are \emph{disjoint}: the
off-diagonal zeros of one column are covered by another. Only the combined chain
(column~4) is uniformly green, because the boolean composition of complementary
detectors is what closes the coverage gap---this is the mechanism behind the
$0.40/0.57/0.59 \rightarrow 0.83$ recall jump, not a better classifier. The single
remaining cool cell, out-of-scope clinical advice ($0.44$), is the hardest class:
disguised dosing/diagnosis requests with neither a lexical nor a PHI marker, where
only the higher-latency LLM scope judge (\S\ref{sec:hcpanel}) applies. This
heatmap is the empirical argument for the declarative, multi-detector design of
Figure~\ref{fig:spec}: a deployer composes the columns their threat model needs.

\paragraph{Relationship to HAARF.}
This work operationalizes the Healthcare AI Agents Regulatory Framework
(HAARF)~\cite{haarf2026}. HAARF defines the security-verification \emph{standard}
a clinical AI agent must meet---scope restriction, minimum-necessary access, PHI
protection, and auditable refusal. QFIRE is a runtime \emph{enforcement} and
\emph{measurement} layer for those requirements: each HAARF control maps to a
declarative rule or chain (e.g.\ minimum-necessary $\rightarrow$
\texttt{hc\_phi\_other\_patient\_record}; no-diagnosis $\rightarrow$
\texttt{hc\_no\_diagnosis}), and the immutable audit log supplies the evidence
trail HAARF verification expects. The HealthBench result quantifies why such a
framework cannot rely on a generic injection classifier alone: the controls HAARF
most cares about are precisely the threats that carry no injection signal.

\paragraph{Why a declarative language matters in regulated settings.}
The baselines encode policy in Python and model weights; QFIRE encodes it in
version-controlled YAML the engine interprets (Figure~\ref{fig:spec}). In a
clinical deployment this is not a convenience but a compliance property: a hospital
security officer can read, diff, review, and unit-test a chain
(\texttt{qfire rules test} runs every rule's exemplars) without modifying or
trusting the engine internals, and changes to policy are auditable in version
control. The positive-security results of this paper are only deployable because
the policy that produced them is inspectable data.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
QFIRE's positive-security stance trades permissiveness for safety: a strict
healthcare chain can over-block benign clinical-adjacent prompts, which we
measure (\S\ref{sec:hcpanel}) rather than hide. On generic injection a strong
classifier (PromptGuard-2) matches QFIRE; QFIRE's measured advantage is the
scope/PHI coverage of \S\ref{sec:healthbench} and single-binary deployment with no
Python in the hot path, not a generic-detector win. Cross-dataset detection
numbers are lower than a detector's in-distribution test score, underscoring that
single-corpus claims do not transfer; we therefore report a public, mixed corpus
and release the snapshot. The LLM-judge node's cost grows with the number of scope
rules, motivating the cheap-before-expensive ordering. Our PHI matchers are
conservative for identifiers (names, biometrics, face photos) that are not reliably
recoverable from text alone. Finally, the LLM scope judge inherits the biases and
failure modes of its backing model; we mitigate but do not eliminate this by
keeping deterministic detectors ahead of it and by reporting judge-chain
over-blocking explicitly.

\section{Conclusion}
QFIRE shows that a Rust, parallel, positive-security firewall can combine
low-latency local inference, de-obfuscation, and scope constraining in one
reproducible toolchain. All artifacts and the \texttt{make paper} pipeline are
released for independent verification.
